A Daily Reset Button

Instilling calmness is not a luxury, it's a necessity.

You cannot imagine the craving for rest that I feel — a hunger and thirst. For six long days, since my work was done, my mind has been a whirlpool, swift, unprogressive and incessant, a torrent of thoughts leading nowhere, spinning round swift and steady. — H.G. Wells

I’m an only child who thrives on solitude.

As I get older, carving out moments to myself have been a challenge: there are dishes that require washing, laundry that requires folding, errands that require running.

It’s easy to get bogged down by the mundane, but should we undergo these actions for too long without balancing them with activities that foster calmness and strengthen our inner resolve, we’ll end up bitter, irritable, and disconnected from the present.

Below are 5 activities that help me reset, and I commit to doing at least one each day. It doesn’t matter what time during the day or for how long (although 45–60 minutes is ideal), so long as it’s done. Whether I’m having a pleasant or a trying day matters none.

Walking. While taking care of a dog may be viewed as burdensome, I revel in the fact that part of my responsibilities involve spending time outdoors with my pupper. We’re fortunate to live near two parks and a beautiful 9 km trail of green space for walkers, runners, and cyclists, all of which serve as an escape from the noisy concrete jungle of Toronto. Not only does my body crave a nice walk after spending at least eight hours sitting in front of a computer screen, but research shows that going for walks specifically in nature boots attention and rejuvenates the brain. Depending on the weather and dinner plans, I either take my dog for a prolonged walk as soon as I return from work or after our dinners. Put down the remote control, get up off the couch, and go for an evening walk instead.

Running. Although there is an infinite number of ways that one can exercise, I am partial to running. Plain and simple, I enjoy the flexibility that running brings, how little is required in order to be a runner, and the fact that I don’t need a teammate. For me, all I need is music, headphones, and a pair of running shoes. That’s it. That’s all. I have run in the morning. I have run late at night. I have gone for a run in the freezing cold and when the sun has been blazing hot. The only competition you face is your limits. How far are you able to run? How long are you able to keep the pace? Are you able to meet your running goal despite the difficult weather? It’s only you and perfect solitude.

Reading. I have been a long-time supporter of reading for 30 minutes before heading to bed, but recently I’ve been experimenting with reading as soon as I wake up. I make a fresh cup of coffee in my french press, let out my dog while it steeps, and then pour my first cup and sit down with a book. I’ve noticed that reading in the morning facilitates a calmness that continues as I head into the office at 8:30 a.m. There’s also something to be said of doing something that doesn’t involve staring at a screen when we first wake up.

Listening. I’m not ashamed to admit that I have a finite amount of motivation. Although 80% of the work I do is meaningful, there are still days where I drag my feet. For this, I use audiobooks and podcasts. I even have a selection of favourite podcast episodes that I listen to that gives me an extra mood boost. During the day, especially on my commute to and from work, listening to people much wiser than me does the trick.

Eating. When was the last time that you took yourself out to dinner? It’s been a few months for me. I remember it clearly: My partner was away and I had nowhere to be after work. I decided, on a whim, to visit a new public library that was a few subway stops away from my apartment. I meandered through the aisles and found two books that I had been meaning to read. Instead of taking the subway, I decided to take the long way home and walk outside. On my way, I paused in front of my favourite sushi joint in the neighbourhood and impulsively decided to eat dinner there. At a table for one, I sat, ate, and read. Nowhere to be. Nothing to attend to. All it took was 2 hours alone, $15.00, and a library book to feel renewed.

Taking the time to reset every day isn’t selfish, it’s a matter of self-sufficiency. To be able to develop resiliency, fortitude, and endurance for life’s unending challenges. To neither burn out or fade away. To welcome each day with energy and enthusiasm. To ensure that we are living up to our potential.

Life is a marathon not a sprint. Commit to its longevity.

John Thomas Smith
